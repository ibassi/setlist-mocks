Know every song that played.

Wherever the music is coming from — a DJ, a bar, a party, someone else's aux — start Catchy when it starts. It listens along and names what it hears, hour after hour, and is honest about the bits it couldn't. Afterwards the whole thing is yours: a tracklist, a timeline of what played, and a playlist to send your friends.

Shazam catches one song. Catchy catches everything that played.

step 1 · while the music is playing

Start it once, then tap for what matters.

Tap the disc for any song you have to have — that's the catch that always lands, and it works straight from the lock screen without unlocking. In between, leave the phone where it can hear and it keeps adding what it can pick up on its own. Anything it can't name is still marked, with the moment saved, so nothing disappears quietly.

  • It keeps going with the screen off. Lock the phone, use other apps — the set keeps running, and it says so on your lock screen. Film a video and the camera takes the microphone, so the set parks itself and picks back up when you come back.
  • There's a Catch now button on the lock screen. Hear something you need the name of, press it without unlocking, and roughly fifteen seconds later it has an answer.
  • No signal is fine. It keeps collecting and names everything the moment you're back online — No service — still capturing.
  • When it misses, it says so — too muffled to name — try it out of your pocket — instead of pretending it heard nothing.
step 2 · whenever you get to it

Settle the maybes in a minute.

The sure things are already in the playlist. For everything it was only half-sure about, you hear the exact seconds your phone caught and keep it or drop it — or hit keep all and be done. Moments it couldn't name are tucked at the bottom, honestly labelled, with the clip kept in case you want to hunt the ID later.

step 3 · the set, kept

Everything that played, in order.

Each session becomes a set: the tracklist in order, a timeline you can drag a finger across to see what was playing when, and one tap to a playlist in Spotify or Apple Music. Songs you loved get a heart and land in your library with the set still attached to them.

step 4 · for the group chat

Proof you were there.

Every set renders a card — the name, the length, the timeline of what played, and how many moments stayed a mystery. Send the card and the playlist link together, or drop it straight on a story. Like a Strava run, but for the music.

It listens. Here is exactly what it keeps.

An app that holds a microphone open for hours owes you a straight answer about where the sound goes.

  • The sound itself never leaves. Your phone turns what it hears into a fingerprint on the device and sends only that — never the audio — to Apple's Shazam service to be matched. The audio it fingerprinted is dropped on the spot.
  • Audio is kept only where you'll be asked to judge it — one short clip for each song it was unsure about, and each moment it couldn't name. They stay on your phone, delete themselves when you finish reviewing the set (or after 30 days if you never do), and leave the device only if you deliberately share one. The one exception is a clip you tap keep clip on: that one is yours until you delete it.
  • When a match can't be made right then — no signal, or the service not answering — that stretch of audio waits on the phone until it can be identified, and is deleted the moment it is. That's the price of not losing it.
  • No account today. Capturing, reviewing and exporting need no sign-up, no password and no profile on a server — your sets live on your phone. (The friends features further up would need something to connect people; we'll say so plainly when we get there.)
  • No analytics SDK, no tracking, no location. The only thing our own server ever sees is a random install ID when you export to Spotify — it exists to stop the shared Spotify account being abused, it's tied to nothing about you, and it resets if you reinstall.
